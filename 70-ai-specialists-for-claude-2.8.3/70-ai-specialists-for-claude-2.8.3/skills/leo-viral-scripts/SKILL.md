## Where Your Viewers Come From (start here, always)

Every other decision hangs off this one. Figure out where the views come from first, then choose the play. You can see the actual split for any video in YouTube Studio under the *Reach* tab — it tells you exactly which of these is sending people.

- **Homepage and subscriptions feed** (YouTube calls it "Browse") — this is people scrolling, not searching. You're competing on a thumbnail that pops and a title that sparks curiosity, against everything else YouTube could show them. Packaging is everything here. Open with a bang, hold back the payoff, and sell the question in their head. YouTube picks these based on what each person already watches, so you're fighting for *their* attention, not trying to rank for a search.
- **Suggested videos** — the sidebar and the "up next." Your job here is simply to be the obvious next thing to watch after a bigger video. Match its audience, touch the same topic, be the natural sequel.
- **Search** — someone typed a question and wants the answer. So **name the answer right in the title and give it to them fast.** Lead with the keyword, deliver the payoff early. This is the *opposite* of the homepage play: hold back here and people bounce straight to the next result.
- **Shared links** (off YouTube — texts, posts, emails) — no algorithm helping you, and no context. Go for a clear title over a clever one, and assume the viewer knows nothing.

**The number-one reason a good video flops is using the wrong play for where the views are coming from.** A curiosity title on a search video starves; a keyword-stuffed title on a homepage video gets scrolled right past.

## Rules of Thumb (guides, not guarantees)

These are *compared to your own channel's normal*, never fixed promises. The numbers shift, so always check them against your own Studio data, not someone else's figure.

- **Click-through rate (CTR — the share of people who click when YouTube shows them your video).** It usually lands somewhere in the low single digits — think the 2-10% range, most channels clustering low. It runs *higher in Search than on the homepage*, and swings a lot by niche and channel size. Use your own channel's average as the bar, not a number from someone else. Find it in Studio under Reach → impressions CTR.
- **The first 30 seconds matter most.** A great click only counts if people stay. A high click rate paired with a sharp drop in the first 15-30 seconds tells YouTube the title or thumbnail overpromised — and it quietly stops showing the video around. Win the intro or lose the reach. (More on this in *Where It Goes Wrong* below.)
- **A breakout video is one that's way above your channel's usual** — a useful bar is roughly 3-10× your normal views. Those are the only videos worth studying closely and copying, because something in the packaging clearly worked. Measure against *your* normal, not a fixed number.
- **Front-load the key word in your title.** Titles can run to 100 characters, but feeds and search cut them off well before that — and *phones cut them off earliest*, often around the first 40-60 characters. So put the hook word, and any number or bracket, right at the front. Check your channel's device split in Studio, since a phone-heavy audience gets cut off sooner.
- **Your thumbnail has to read at phone size** — the size of a thumbnail in someone's hand. Keep it to about 3 things max: one face with one clear emotion, and a strong contrast between the subject and the background.
- **Around 8+ minutes lets you place ads mid-video** — that's roughly the length where a mid-video ad break becomes worthwhile. Only go longer when the video genuinely earns the extra time.

## Frameworks

- **Package first** — the title and thumbnail are one team, and I write them *before* the script. They should say *different* things — never repeat the title's words on the thumbnail. Test the package as an idea first: *would a total stranger, who's never heard of this channel, click this while scrolling?* Best of all, take a title that already broke out in your niche and rework it closely, rather than inventing from scratch.

- **How I build the thumbnail** (a way of thinking, not a checklist) — the **expression test**: one face, one unmistakable emotion. About 3 things max on it. Strong contrast between subject and background. Spark curiosity with something that looks a little *off* — an element that shouldn't be there. Keep on-thumbnail text to 4 words or fewer, or none at all. And always make **2-3 versions** so YouTube can test them against each other — never ship one and cross your fingers.

- **Find the leak: click → content → next video** — three layers, three things to check, three different fixes. If your **click rate** is low, the problem is packaging (title and thumbnail). If people **click but drop off partway** (your average watch time and the shape of the drop-off graph), the problem is the content or the pacing. If they finish but **don't start another video**, the problem is your ending and screen-end links. Work out which layer is leaking *before* you rewrite anything.

- **Reading the drop-off graph** — look at the shape, then fix by zone. A **steep first-30-seconds drop** means the hook isn't paying off the title fast enough. A **dip that then flattens out** is healthy — protect that flat stretch. A **sudden cliff in the middle** is one specific boring bit — cut it or shake it up. A **spike back above 100%** means a moment people rewound to rewatch — pull that moment earlier or tease it up front.

- **The first-30-seconds promise** — for homepage and entertainment videos: confirm the click (pay off what the title promised) within about 10 seconds, raise the real stakes, and open a question that pulls them forward. No "hey guys," no slow warm-up. *For search and tutorial videos this flips — see When the Rules Reverse.*

- **Keep questions open** — never rely on a single curiosity gap. Have one big question running the whole video (that's the title's promise), plus smaller ones every 60-90 seconds. Each one only gets answered as the next one opens, so there's never a clean place to click away.

- **End by sending them to the next video** — the strongest "do this next" is the *next watch*: a screen-end link to a specific video they'll actually click (ideally that rewatch moment, or a natural follow-on). Asking for a subscribe comes second. YouTube rewards videos that keep people watching, so *getting them into the next video is the move that really pays off over time.*

## Where It Goes Wrong (the warning signs and the fixes)

- **The promise the video can't keep** — a title or thumbnail that oversells. *Sign:* a burst of clicks, then people leave in the first 15-30 seconds; YouTube stops showing it around once it sees that early drop. *Why it stings:* YouTube reads "lots of clicks, fast exit" as overselling, and pulls back. *Fix:* make the title a promise the first 30 seconds actually keeps.
- **The wrong people clicking** — packaging that pulls clicks from folks who won't stick around, which then confuses who YouTube shows it to next. *Sign:* high click rate, low watch time, traffic from odd places. *Fix:* package for the viewer you actually want, not the widest possible crowd.
- **Winning one video, hurting the next** — squeezing every click out of one video at the cost of whether people keep watching afterward. *Sign:* your *next* video gets shown less. *Fix:* optimize for the whole watching session, not the single click.
- **The fake curiosity gap** — a hook that turns out to be a bait-and-switch. Over time it wears down how YouTube trusts the channel. *Fix:* whatever question you open, answer it honestly inside the video.

## When the Rules Reverse (the exceptions)

- **Tutorials and search videos** — NAME the answer in the title and give it early. Don't hold back. People searching "how to X" want X, fast.
- **Evergreen, search-friendly content** — favor the keyword people actually search over a clever curiosity line; lasting traffic beats a one-day spike.
- **The hold-back-the-payoff opening** — that's a homepage/entertainment move only. It *kills* how-to and search videos.

## Worked Example

> **Brief (made up):** mid-size finance channel (~40k subs), idea "how I track my spending." Goal: more subscribers. Proof the user gave: this method cut their own spending about £300/month.
>
> **First call — where do the views come from?** People literally *type* "how to track spending." So this is a **Search** video, not a homepage one. That means the hold-back-the-payoff instinct is wrong here. I **flip it**: name the tracker and the £300 result right in the title, and deliver it inside the first ~20 seconds — because holding back starves a search video.
>
> **The plan:** a search-led video built to win subscribers. The promise is the result, delivered early; subscribers come from "let's do this together every month."
>
> **Packaging:** search/mix title — *"How I Track Spending in One Spreadsheet (Cut £300/Month)"* — keyword up front, result named. A curiosity alt to re-push on the homepage later — *"The £300 Leak Hiding in Your Bank App."* Thumbnails (2-3 to test): (a) face mid-realisation + "£300?!" + the spreadsheet; (b) phone banking app with one row circled in red, no face. Test both.
>
> **Hook (0-20s):** *"If you want to know exactly where your money goes, here's the one-spreadsheet system that showed me I was leaking about £300 a month — and I'll show you the exact leak in a second."* (Answer named, payoff promised fast — right for Search.)
>
> **Graph plan:** have the spreadsheet set up by ~0:45 (so there's no boring cliff), then name the actual £300 leak at ~2:30 as the rewatch moment. **Ending:** screen-end link to "I do this breakdown every month" → sends them to the next video *and* earns the subscribe.

The "where do the views come from" call flips the whole script: a homepage-style slow open would have tanked this one. Search wants the answer up front, paid off honestly.
