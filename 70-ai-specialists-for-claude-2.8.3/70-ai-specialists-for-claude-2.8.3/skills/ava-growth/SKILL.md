## How each app decides what to show (the one distinction everything hangs on)

Each app pushes your video to more people based on a different main signal. So first we work out which app you're on, then we pull the lever that matters most there.

- **TikTok** — it mostly watches how long people stay and whether they finish (and share or save). It shows a new video to a small group first, then keeps widening if that group reacts well. It leans on **your existing followers** finishing it before it goes wider. *Pull this lever first:* getting people to watch to the end. Keep it short (under 15s), loop it so it feels seamless, cut fast. The bar for "good enough to push wider" keeps rising over time, so compare yourself to your own past videos, not a fixed number.
- **Reels** — the strongest signals are how long people watch, how often they DM it to a friend (Instagram calls this "sends per reach"), and how many like it relative to how many saw it. Sends to friends are the best way to reach **new** people; watch time gets the first push going. *Pull this lever first:* give people a reason to send it to a friend **and** a hook that holds attention — a relatable callout, a save-worthy list, or a "tag someone who."
- **Shorts** — inside YouTube Studio there's a number called **"Viewed vs Swiped Away,"** and people swiping away in the first moment is what kills your reach. Heads up: Shorts view counts run high — almost any playback counts as a "view" — so judge by your view rate and swipe-away, not the big view number. *Pull this lever first:* nail the very first frame and first second so people don't swipe before the video even loads.

**Getting seen is not the same as getting followed.** These apps are built to get your video *seen* (watch time and shares) — they are not built to get you *followers*. A follow is a choice a person makes, and you have to earn it on purpose: with a series they want to come back for, a recognisable you, and a clear "here's what you get if you follow." Think of reach and follows as two separate jobs, because they are.

## Frameworks

- **The Three-Gate Audit** — every video has to clear three gates, in order: the *Stop* (does the first second on Reels, or first 3 seconds on TikTok, actually stop the scroll?), the *Stay* (does it hold people to the end?), and the *Spread* (does the ending make someone send it, save it, or rewatch it?). Let's find which gate is failing before we fix anything. A big drop-off in the first second or two is almost always a hook problem — read it against your own past videos, not a fixed number. And a clean loop with no dead air can push your average watch time over 100%, because people rewatch.

- **Searchable vs Servable** — a *servable* video rides the feed for a few days on watch time, then fades. A *searchable* video keeps getting found for months because it answers a search — so you put the same search phrase in your spoken hook, your on-screen text, and your caption. **One catch:** searchable plays work best on TikTok and Shorts (YouTube search shows Shorts). Reels search is weaker, so on Reels lean toward servable videos and sends-to-friends instead.

- **Outlier Mining** — find videos that did way better than that creator's usual (lots of views relative to their follower count), then copy the *part that travels* — the hook structure, the framing, the emotional beat — not the topic itself. Borrow the mechanic, swap in your own subject. *Watch out:* if that video only blew up because of a trending sound or a one-off news moment, the pattern won't carry over for you.

- **The 3C Content Mix** — *Core* (your main niche topics that turn viewers into buyers), *Culture* (trends you put your own spin on), and *Connection* (personal, behind-the-scenes, the stuff that builds a real bond). All Core and your reach stalls; all Culture and nobody converts; all Connection and you never reach new people. **One exception: ignore this completely during cold-start (below).**

## Named techniques (when they work, when they backfire)

- **The hook / open loop / pattern interrupt** (the Stop gate) — opening with a question that needs answering, a curiosity gap, or a visual that breaks the scroll. *Backfires when it's bait:* a shocking opener with no real payoff spikes your views but kills your follows, and it teaches the app to show you to the wrong crowd.
- **Retention editing** (the Stay gate) — cut on movement, leave no dead air, and re-hook at the loop point on videos under 10 seconds. *This always applies — it's the cheapest way to get people to finish.*
- **Your first frame is your cover photo** (Reels) — that opening frame is what shows in your grid. If it's a blurry mid-blink shot, people decide to swipe before the sound even starts.

## Sensible starting points

These are starting points, not rules — we'll confirm them against your real analytics.

- **Length:** 7-15s when you want people to finish or loop (TikTok, Reels). 21-34s when you want a save or a send, because you need room for a payoff worth sharing. Both apps allow much longer (Reels and Shorts run up to about 3 minutes) — use the length your payoff actually needs, not the maximum.
- **How often to post:** during a growth push, a workable target is around 1-2x a day on TikTok and 5-7x a week on Reels — treat that as something to test, not gospel. **One caveat:** once you hit the point where you're burning out, steady posting and better videos beat sheer volume. And never delete weak posts to "start fresh" — deleting resets nothing and can actually hurt you.
- **Trends:** small trends can die in days; trending *sounds* usually last longer — roughly one to three weeks, sometimes more for meme-able audio. The takeaway: jump on a sound while it's still climbing, not after it's everywhere. A trend that's already dead just makes your account look stale, so skip it.

## Cold-Start Playbook (newer accounts — under ~50 posts, low followers)

This is where beginners lose the most ground, and it overrides the 3C and trend advice above.

- **Stick to one clear topic for your first ~10 videos** so the app learns who to show you to. A mixed bag this early gives it no clear audience to find.
- **Don't chase trends yet** — trend reach brings a random crowd that won't stick around, and it confuses the app about who you're for.
- **Pick one repeatable format** so both the app and a brand-new follower know exactly what they're getting from you.

## Worked Example

> **The brief (made up for illustration):** A B2B coach for fractional CFOs, on Reels, 3k followers. One video hit 40k views but only got 19 follows from it. Everything else sits around 600 views. Goal: followers who turn into discovery calls.
>
> **Diagnosis:** The obvious read is "this is a follows/profile problem — fix the bio." That's wrong here. 40k views with only 19 follows is really a **wrong-audience problem (a Stop-gate problem) wearing a follows-problem costume.** That viral video's hook ("this money mistake is costing you") pulled a big, cheap-curiosity crowd — not founders who'd actually hire a CFO. The giveaway isn't the ratio alone; it's *who showed up in the comments and the follower stats*: jokes and "lol" from regular consumers, not "what's your rate?" A sharper bio can't convert people who were never your buyer in the first place.
>
> **Strategy:** (1) Re-aim the hook so it filters for the right person — *"If your startup is past $1M in revenue and you're still doing the books in a spreadsheet..."* — fewer views, but far more of the right people following. (2) Build a **series** ("CFO Teardown Tuesdays") so the right viewer follows to catch the next one — that's the follow lever, and it's separate from reach. (3) Make the bio a clear promise with a clear payoff: *"Fractional CFO playbooks for $1M-$10M founders. New teardown every week."*
>
> **Content Direction:** A searchable piece (going light on Reels here, since Reels search is weak) — repurpose the teardown onto TikTok with "how to read a P&L" in the hook, on-screen text, and caption. A servable Reel — a 12s "spot the cash-flow trap in this statement" with a "send this to your co-founder" line, because sends-to-friends is the lever for reaching new people.

The win came from refusing the default move — we diagnosed a wrong-audience problem instead of blaming the bio.

## Where things go wrong

- **Bait hooks** that pull the wrong crowd — they spike views, kill follows, and teach the app to keep showing you to people who'll never buy.
- **Jumping on a sound too late** — once it's everywhere, the reach boost is gone and you just look behind.
- **Chasing a big follower number** over how many viewers actually follow you — and **buying followers**, which wrecks your engagement numbers and quietly suppresses your future reach.
- **Deleting and reposting to "reset"** — it's a myth. It resets nothing and throws away the momentum that post had already built.
- **Treating Shorts' big view counts as real reach** — almost any playback counts, so judge Shorts by view rate and swipe-away instead.
- **A sudden reach drop across the board:** if *every* video tanks at once (not just one dud), that usually means your reach has been limited — a flagged sound, a banned hashtag, a guideline strike, or a link-in-bio that broke the rules. Let's check this *before* touching your content, because no hook fixes a reach limit.
