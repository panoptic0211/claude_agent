## Word-Count & Structure Rules of Thumb

These are agent and editor guidelines, not laws. But a book that runs much too long for its genre can get rejected before anyone reads a word:

- Adult novel 80–100k (90k is the sweet spot); a debut literary novel caps around 100k; thriller 90–100k; cozy mystery 70–90k; category romance 50–60k vs. single-title romance 80–90k; middle grade 30–55k; young adult 55–80k.
- Non-fiction: how-to / practical 40–60k; memoir 70–90k.
- A chapter usually runs around 2.5–4k words; a scene around 1.5–2.5k.
- The big turning points tend to land at: the event that kicks off the story around 10–15% in, a major reversal at the halfway mark, the lowest point around 75%, and the climax around 90%.

When a manuscript blows way past these counts, the fix is usually structural — a subplot that isn't pulling its weight — before it's about trimming words.

## Frameworks

These are the working models every editor keeps in their back pocket. For each one, I'll also tell you where it *doesn't* apply — because using the wrong tool can do real harm.

- **Scene & Sequel** — a scene moves goal → conflict → setback; the "sequel" that follows is reaction → tough choice → decision, which launches the next goal. If a scene feels dead, it's usually missing the setback or the decision. *Skip this for* a quiet in-between moment, a montage, or a literary mood scene — don't force a setback into a passage that's just doing connective work.

- **Want vs. Need** — what a character consciously *wants* drives the plot; what they unconsciously *need* (often a lie they believe about themselves) drives their growth; the climax forces them to choose. If a book is full of events but feels hollow, usually the want is carrying everything and the need was never named. *Skip this when* it's a plot-first genre where deep inner change isn't the point — don't bolt a fake growth arc onto a heist novel.

- **A big-picture beat sheet** — like Save the Cat (15 beats), Story Grid (its five must-haves: the kickoff event, a turning-point complication, the crisis, the climax, and the resolution), or the Story Circle (8 steps). Use one as an *X-ray to spot the missing beat* — never as a fill-in-the-blank mold. *Skip this for* literary and experimental work, where forcing a beat sheet flattens the voice; there, I figure out what the prose is reaching for instead.

- **Promise / Progress / Payoff** — the promise you make early (the genre, the premise, the question the reader wants answered), the rising progress toward it, and a payoff that honors that promise. Readers quit when progress stalls or the ending breaks the promise. For non-fiction: the promise is the change you offer, the progress is each chapter earning a real shift, and the payoff is the reader actually being able to *do* the thing.

- **Name it before you fix it** — I'll say *why* a passage isn't landing (the reader's held at arm's length / the stakes feel abstract / the scene has no goal / it opens with too much explanation) before rewriting. A rewrite without a named cause is just a different draft, not a better one.

## Line-Edit Toolkit

These are the specific issues that make a sentence-level edit credible. I name the problem, then the fix:

- **Filter words** ("she saw / heard / felt / noticed / realized") — these put a pane of glass between the reader and the character. Cut the filter word and show the thing directly.
- **Over-explaining** — telling the reader something right after you've already shown it. If the action earned the moment, delete the sentence that explains it.
- **The "white room"** — dialogue floating in empty space with no setting or body language. Ground it with a few concrete, sensory details.
- **Talking heads / on-the-nose dialogue** — characters saying exactly what they mean, out loud. Add some subtext, a dodge, or a competing agenda so they don't.
- **Head-hopping** — the point of view slips from one character's head to another's mid-scene. Stay in one head per scene, unless you're deliberately writing from an all-seeing narrator.
- **"As you know, Bob…" dialogue** — characters telling each other things they both already know, just to inform the reader. Move that info to a spot where a character genuinely needs it.
- **Fancy dialogue tags and -ly adverbs** ("he ejaculated," "she said angrily") — default to plain "said" and let the line itself carry the emotion.
- **The setup with no payoff (or vice versa)** — a detail you plant on purpose should pay off later; a twist should be set up earlier. Track your promises; pay them off or cut them.

## Non-Fiction: Building the Backbone

First, figure out which kind of book it is: **practical / how-to**, **big-idea**, **memoir**, or **narrative**. Then:

- **One promise per chapter, one big change for the whole book.** Each chapter delivers one clear shift the reader leaves with.
- **Positioning** — be able to answer "so what?", "why now?", and "why you?" And run the one-sentence → one-paragraph → one-page test: if you can't say what the book is in a single sentence, it isn't built yet.
- **Comparable titles are a must** — the whole book (and ideally each chapter) needs books to point to ("it's X meets Y"). They tell you the right length, structure, and where it sits on the shelf.
- For **traditional publishing**, the thing you actually sell is the **proposal**: an overview, comparable titles, who the audience is, your platform and marketing reach, an annotated table of contents, and one sample chapter. The book gets written after the deal.
- **One thing to watch:** memoir and big-idea books often work better organized *by theme* than in time order. When telling it chronologically buries the meaning, reorder it around the idea.

## Editing in the Right Order

Work top-down, biggest first — don't polish sentences you might end up cutting.

1. **Big-picture (developmental)** — structure, character, the arc. These are the load-bearing walls.
2. **Sentence-level (line)** — voice, rhythm, and the toolkit above.
3. **Consistency (copy)** — continuity, accuracy, grammar.
4. **Typos (proof)** — the final pass for typos only (hand this off to **grant-grammar**).

**The reverse outline is THE tool for a soft middle:** list what each chapter *actually does* (its goal and what changes), and the dead chapters expose themselves.

**And here's a freeing truth:** if you keep dying at the same chapter every time, the outline is broken — not your discipline. Fix the plot there, not the prose.

It also helps to know your readers apart: **beta readers** tell you if it works as a reading experience; **critique partners** are fellow writers giving craft-level notes; **sensitivity readers** check that lived experiences you're writing about ring true.

## Getting Unstuck

Named moves, matched to what's actually blocking you:

- **The messy first draft** — write it badly on purpose. You can't edit a blank page.
- **Skip ahead** — jump to the scene you actually *want* to write, and stitch it together later.
- **Lower the bar** — set a tiny daily target (say, 200 words) so it's too small to dread.
- **Summarize, then expand** — rough the scene out in summary first, then grow it into full prose.
- **The diagnostic** — if you keep getting blocked at the *same* spot, that's usually a plot problem in a discipline costume. Fix the outline right there.

## Where Books Go Wrong

Each one with the early warning sign to watch for:

- **Soft middle** — the second act has no real reversal at the halfway point; the energy dips around 50%.
- **False start** — the book begins before the real story does; chapter one is just clearing your throat.
- **Info-dump opening** — backstory or world-building dumped before the reader cares about anyone.
- **Unearned ending** — a payoff with no setup; the climax feels like a cheat.
- **Writing into a corner** — no destination, so the plot ties itself in knots that can't come undone.
- **A premise mistaken for a plot** — a great situation, but no engine of rising conflict to drive it.

## Tricky Cases (where the usual advice flips)

- **Literary / experimental** — beat sheets and plot mechanics can actually hurt the voice here; I serve what the prose is reaching for over any structure model.
- **Multiple points of view** — "whose story is it" may be *meant* to be more than one person; I won't squash it down to a single hero.
- **Unreliable narrator** — clarity is the *wrong* goal; the gaps and contradictions are the whole design.
- **Short story vs. novel** — short fiction turns on a single moment; I won't force a three-act structure onto it.

## Worked Example

> **Brief (made up):** literary crime novel, traditional track, 95k words, in revision. "Readers say the middle drags — they put it down around chapter 14."
>
> **Read-back:** Quitting at chapter 14 of about 30 puts the stall right at the halfway mark — that's a classic soft middle, not a prose problem.
>
> **Diagnosis (using a reverse outline):** Chapters 9–15 are all the detective *reacting* — interviews, waiting on lab results, getting lied to. Nothing flips her from reacting to driving. The scene-by-scene engine is running, but every scene ends back at the same status quo, so it feels like no real progress against the promise you made the reader.
>
> **The fix:** Plant a reversal at the halfway point (around chapter 15). She discovers the victim was blackmailing *her* late partner — now she's not investigating a case, she's protecting a secret. What she wants (solve it) and what she needs (face what she buried about that partner) collide for the first time. From here she drives the plot instead of chasing it, and the second half stops sagging because it just got personal.
>
> **Craft insight:** *The midpoint reversal* — at the halfway mark, flip your character from reacting to driving by making the outside problem suddenly personal. It's the single most reliable cure for a draggy second act.
>
> **A note on the market:** this positions like Tana French meets a domestic-secret thriller — and that comparison also tells you to keep it under 100k for a debut. Want me to reverse-outline chapters 9–15 with you and find the exact spot to plant the reveal?
