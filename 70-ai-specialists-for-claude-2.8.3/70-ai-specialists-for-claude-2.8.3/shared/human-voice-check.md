# Human Voice Check — shared anti-AI-tells checklist

Every writing specialist reads this before returning a draft. Vocabulary and tone are only half the job; this file handles the other half: **rhythm and structure** that make even good writing read as AI.

**Key principle: the tell is pattern density, not single words.** A device used once is human and sharp. The same device repeated becomes a machine cadence. Scan for the *device repeating*, not just for a banned word.

**Always match the user's own voice.** Use the voice, vocabulary, and rhythm from their Business Profile and from any samples or material they've shared. The goal is that it sounds like *they* wrote it — never a generic "AI voice" and never an imposed house style.

## Scan 1 — vocabulary kill-list
Replace these with a concrete noun or an active verb.
- **Verbs:** delve, leverage, foster, ignite, empower, uncover, unleash, underscore, optimise, streamline, elevate, navigate, unlock.
- **Adjectives:** cutting-edge, seamless, robust, future-ready, multifaceted, pivotal, dynamic, comprehensive, transformative, revolutionary, game-changer.
- **Flowery metaphors:** tapestry, landscape, realm, beacon, journey, roadmap, symphony, ecosystem.
- Example fix: "We empower users to optimise their workflows" → "We help you work faster."

## Scan 2 — rhythm tells (the important scan)
1. **Stacked balanced antithesis.** "It's not X, it's Y" / "used to be X, now Y" / "from X to Y." One is punchy. Three-plus in a piece is a detectable cadence. Keep the 1–2 that carry the main point; flatten the rest into plain statements.
2. **Em-dash overuse.** The single most-cited AI tell. Dashes for a real aside are fine — aim for roughly one every 120–150 words, not one a line. Convert the rest to full stops or commas.
3. **Rule of three / triadic parallelism.** "Faster, cleaner, smarter" or "the X vanishes, the Y flattens, the Z…". Punchy once; robotic when every section does it. Vary or merge.
4. **Fortune-cookie maxims.** Symmetrical abstract lines ("when X becomes free, Y becomes priceless"). Allow at most ONE per piece, as a deliberate pull-quote. No pile-ups.
5. **Low burstiness.** Uniform sentence length and "rectangle" paragraphs (every paragraph three sentences of ~15–20 words). Break it: follow a long explanatory sentence with a short punch.
6. **Keyword looping.** Repeating the same key noun many times. Vary the wording.

## Also check (less common, still worth a pass)
- **Hedging:** "it's worth noting," "it's important to consider," "generally speaking," "aims to." Cut it — take the stance.
- **Opener clichés:** "In today's fast-paced world," "let's dive in," "picture this," "imagine a world."
- **Fake-hook transitions:** "But here's the kicker," "That's only half the story," "Here's the truth," "Real talk." Cut.
- **Connector clichés:** furthermore, moreover, additionally, in conclusion, ultimately, in essence.
- **Fake experience:** "As a business owner, you know…" + a generic problem. Replace with a specific, real detail.
- **Listicle sameness:** "Bold Header: explanatory sentence" repeated identically. Mix the formatting.
- **Uniform polish:** every paragraph equally developed, no aside, no human mess. Let one breathe.

## The two-question human test
1. Would a real person say this out loud to a friend? (the pub test)
2. Is the rhythm jagged — short punches mixed with long lines — or metronome-even?

## How to apply
After drafting, scan once for vocabulary, then once for *repeated devices* — the second scan matters most. Breaking ~3 repeated devices and thinning the em-dashes is usually 90% of the cleanup. If a check fails, revise before returning.
