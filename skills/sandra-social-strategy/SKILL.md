## Frameworks

Every platform has ONE thing it cares about most when it decides who sees your post. The plan bends to that one thing, not just to your goal.

- **What each platform rewards most**:
  - **IG Reels** → *shares + watch time*. The biggest signals are how long people watch, how often they send it to someone (shares), and likes — and *sends* (people sharing it in DMs) are the real lever that gets you in front of people who don't follow you yet, counting for far more than likes. Reels get shown to a small group first; if enough of them watch it through and send it on, it gets pushed to a bigger audience. So make content people want to send to a friend.
  - **TikTok** → *how long people watch, how many finish, and how many rewatch*. Average watch time and finish rate matter most, with shares and saves counting for more than likes. Make it loop and pay off fast — a clean loop quietly racks up rewatches.
  - **YouTube Shorts** → *how fast people swipe away* is the thing that hurts you most. Whether people watch or swipe past is the tell. Put your best stuff in the very first second — no slow intro, no logo animation.
  - **LinkedIn** → *how long people read it, plus real comments in the first hour*. That first hour mostly decides how far it travels, and comments count for far more than likes. Share one or two strong opinions, lean text-heavy, and ask a question that earns real replies — not "great post" one-liners.
  - **X video** → *how many finish it*. Keep it short, punchy, captioned.

  These can shift over time. The main thing each platform rewards stays pretty stable, but before you bet a whole plan on a specific detail, it's worth a quick check against the platform's own creator help pages.

- **Match the plan to your stage** — there's no one-size-fits-all post mix; it changes as you grow. Treat these as starting points and adjust to what your own numbers show:
  - **Just starting (under ~1-5k):** about 70% broad/reach posts + 20% showing you know your stuff + 10% asking for the sale. You don't have an audience to protect yet, so go for reach. Lean on trends, broad openers, don't box yourself into a narrow niche yet.
  - **Growing (~5k-50k):** about 50/30/20. Start tightening your niche and adding a go-to format people follow you for.
  - **Established / making money (50k+):** shift toward showing expertise and asking for the sale, protect your niche, lean on your signature format. Chasing broad reach now just brings in people who won't buy.
  - I'll state the stage default first, then adjust it for your specific brand.

- **Writing openers for the sound-off scroll** — I'll name the type of hook and put a line of text on the very first frame, because most feeds play silent by default:
  - Hook types: *curiosity* (open a loop they need closed), *contrarian* (go against what everyone assumes), *specificity* (a number or result), *negativity* (the mistake or warning), *pattern-interrupt* (something unexpected on screen in the first frame).
  - To keep people watching, I read the drop-off graph: fix the early bail in the first 2-3 seconds (cut dead air, start in the middle of the action) and the mid-video slump (re-grab them or raise the stakes at the halfway dip).
  - I'll aim the hook at the people who'd actually buy from you — a hook so broad it pulls in the wrong crowd quietly hurts your future reach (more on that in Failure Modes).

- **Trend timing** — jumping on a trend only works if you catch the sound or format while it's still climbing, not after it's peaked. A sound already on hundreds of thousands of videos has probably peaked, and you'll show up just as it fades. So check it's still rising before you build around it. And run the quick two-yes test: it fits your brand *voice* AND reaches the *right* audience.

## Defaults & when to break them

The how-often and what's-healthy numbers below are practitioner rules of thumb — calibrate them against what's normal for your own account.

- **How often to post (a floor, not a ceiling):** roughly IG Reels 3-5/wk, LinkedIn 3-5/wk, TikTok 1-3/day, Shorts daily is fine. Staying consistent beats going big once — a steady pace beats a burst then silence.
- **The number to watch = the thing that platform rewards most**, not likes. On Reels, track how often it's sent and saved; on TikTok, watch-through, finish rate, and rewatches; on Shorts, swipe-away; on LinkedIn, how long people read it and how fast comments come in the first hour.
- **Don't post the exact same thing on two platforms the same day** — people who follow you in both places see it twice, and the second one feels like a rerun. Space them 24-48h apart, or change the edit (recut the opener, add native captions, strip any watermark).
- **What "healthy" looks like** (again, compare to your own normal): a Reel that a good chunk of viewers save is doing well; a high send rate predicts reach better than likes do; on LinkedIn, comments in the first hour make or break it. None of these are hard pass/fail lines — work out your own baseline from your analytics.

## Track & adjust (don't judge a format too soon)

A single Reel or Short can keep picking up views for weeks — so judging a format too early mixes fresh posts with ones that haven't finished spreading, and the numbers come out as noise. Here's how I keep it honest:

- **Wait for enough posts:** judge a format only after **3-5 posts**, AND each one has had time to spread (a week or more for Reels/Shorts; LinkedIn settles in about 48-72h).
- **Cut a format** after **2 posts in a row that come in below your normal** once they've had time to spread (compared to your own median, on that platform's main number).
- **Double down within 48h** while a winner is still climbing — post the format again fast, don't wait for the monthly review.
- Watch **who's actually seeing it — followers vs non-followers:** mostly followers means you're stuck in an echo chamber (fix the hook to reach new people); a healthy share of non-followers means you're growing.

## Failure Modes

- **Late to the trending sound:** showing up after a sound has peaked gets you almost no boost. Catch sounds while they're still climbing.
- **Going viral with the wrong crowd:** a broad clickbait hook can blow up with people who'll never buy — and then your next posts do worse, because the platform now keeps showing you to that mismatched crowd. The fix: write hooks that pre-qualify, speaking to your buyer's specific problem instead of to everyone.
- **Reposting with another platform's watermark:** platforms quietly bury content carrying a rival's stamp (like a TikTok watermark on a Reel). Export it clean and recut it for the new platform.
- **Posting again right after a flop:** a weak post can drag down the reach of the next few. After an obvious flop, follow it with your strongest proven format, not another experiment.
- **Your go-to format getting stale:** any format wears out as people get used to it. Refresh the angle or rotate it out before the numbers start sliding.

## Edge Cases (when the usual play is wrong)

- **B2B / LinkedIn:** chasing "viral" is usually the wrong goal — big view counts that lead nowhere vs real prospects. Optimise for the right people reading and commenting, not raw views.
- **Local / service business:** a small *local* Reel can beat a big national one. Optimise for local relevance, not reach.
- **Regulated niches (finance/health):** trending audio plus claims can land you in compliance trouble. I'll flag it; I won't ride the trend or make the claim — that's one for a qualified professional.
- **Launch windows:** during a big push to sell, ease off the reach experiments — protect the audience who's ready to buy instead of chasing broad views that water down their intent.

## Worked Example

> **Brief (hypothetical):** ceramics studio, ~800 IG followers, goal = bookings for weekend classes, IG Reels + TikTok, can film 3 short clips/week.
>
> **The plan in a nutshell:** Brand-new account → go for reach first. On IG the main thing is *sends*; on TikTok it's *finish rate + rewatches*. The bet: a satisfying, loop-friendly format that strangers send to a friend ("we should book this").
>
> **Post mix (just-starting, 70/20/10):** 70% relatable/broad (the mess, the wobbly first bowl — stuff anyone wants to share), 20% expertise (one quick technique fix), 10% asking for the sale ("book this Saturday").
>
> **Go-to format — *Wonky-to-wow*:** first-frame text hook "this almost ended in the bin" (negativity type) over a collapsing pot → the fix (kills the early 2-3s bail by starting mid-collapse) → the finished piece, looped back to the wobble so TikTok racks up a rewatch. End card: "we teach this Saturday."
>
> **How often:** 3 Reels/wk (the low end of the floor — honest to what they can film), reused on TikTok 24h later with a native recut and no watermark.
>
> **How we'll adjust:** judge *Wonky-to-wow* only after 4 posts, each about 10+ days old. If non-follower reach stays high but bookings don't move, the hook's pulling watchers, not buyers — so tighten the sales post's hook to "local pottery class" wording and move the call-to-action to the bio link. If the send rate clears the studio's own strong-post baseline, double down within 48h with a second wonky variant.

The non-obvious move: when you're brand new, we *deliberately* run mostly broad, relatable content and accept that most of those viral viewers won't book yet — the goal is to build an audience we can reach first, then convert. An established studio would do the exact opposite.
