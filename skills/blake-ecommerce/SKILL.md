## The number I actually optimise — earnings per visitor, not just conversion rate

Here's the simple math I use for every store: **Revenue = Visitors × what each visitor is worth.** And what each visitor is worth (call it revenue per visitor) = your conversion rate × your average order value — counted *after* returns and discounts come out.

I aim at revenue per visitor, not raw conversion rate, and here's why that matters: you can boost conversion overnight with a fat discount and quietly lose money on every sale. So the real target is **profit per visit** (what's left after the cost of the product). Any "win" that lifts conversion but shrinks profit per visit is a loss wearing a winner's medal.

## Always split the numbers before you blame a page (non-negotiable)

**Never diagnose off one blended conversion number.** Before touching anything, break it down two ways: **new vs. returning visitors**, and **phone vs. desktop**.

Two patterns hold true almost everywhere. Returning visitors convert far better than first-timers. And phone visitors convert noticeably worse than desktop — phone cart abandonment runs around 80%, desktop closer to two-thirds. So the phone is usually where the leak lives.

Here's the trap: a "conversion drop" is very often just a shift in who's visiting (more new people, more phone traffic) — not a broken page at all. Statisticians call this Simpson's paradox: the blended number moves even though every group stayed the same. Fixing a page when the real cause is a traffic shift is pure busywork.

## Benchmarks I carry (for rough orientation only — always compared to your own store's trend)

These are ballpark ranges to get oriented, never quoted as a fact about your business. I always anchor them to your own history and your specific category:

- **Sitewide conversion rate by category** (treat as a wide band, not a target — these swing a lot): apparel/fashion ~2–3%, beauty/personal care ~3–5%, electronics ~1–2.5%. The all-category average sits around 2.5–3%.
- **Cart abandonment:** around two-thirds to 70% is normal, higher on phones.
- **Checkout abandonment** (people who *start* checkout but don't finish): lower than cart abandonment — size it from your own checkout-start-to-purchase numbers, not a generic figure.
- **Add-to-cart rate:** healthy stores often land in the high single digits, but this swings hard by category and traffic source — measure your own.
- **Share of revenue from email/SMS:** established direct-to-consumer brands often run around 25–30%+ from their own email and SMS lists — directional, not a rule. **Repeat-customer revenue** and **how often people buy again** vary a lot by category (everyday refills vs. big considered buys) — read your own customer data, don't assume.
- **The money math:** a customer's lifetime value being roughly 3× what it cost to win them is the common rule of thumb for a healthy store; **below 1× means stop spending to grow right now.** Aim to earn back the cost of winning a customer within 6–12 months for direct-to-consumer. These are common operator rules of thumb, not laws — always sanity-check against your real profit per sale.

## Frameworks

- **The step-by-step funnel** — track each step: viewed product → added to cart → started cart → started checkout → bought. Then find the step doing worst *compared to its benchmark and its own past*, not just the lowest number. That gap is your biggest recoverable money. Fixing a step that's already healthy is motion, not progress.

- **Checkout-leak check** — when people bail at checkout (and they're past "just browsing"), it's almost always one of a handful of usual suspects: **(1) the extra costs — shipping, tax, fees — feel too high (the #1 reason, every time), (2) they didn't trust the site with their card, or were forced to create an account, (3) checkout was too long or fiddly, (4) delivery looked too slow, (5) they couldn't see the full price upfront.** Check these before you redesign anything. Most checkouts also ask for way more form fields than they need — cutting those down is one of the highest-payoff fixes there is.

- **Reading a product page (the LIFT lens)** — a product page sells on Value (is this worth it?), Relevance (is this for me?), and Clarity (do I get it?), minus Anxiety (can I trust you?) and Distraction (what's pulling me away?). Above the fold must answer *what is it / why should I care / how much / can I trust you* in one glance. Frame the headline around the job the buyer is hiring the product to do, not a list of features.

- **Ranking fixes by the money they make** — score each fix by Impact × Confidence × Ease, where **Impact = the extra profit per month it should add** (not flashy top-line revenue). Tie-breaker: when two fixes look close, pick the one on a **high-traffic, high-baseline step** (checkout, cart) over a low-traffic glamour page — it shows results faster *and* compounds.

- **Check the math before you test** — how many people you need for a fair A/B test depends on how common the action already is and how big a change you're hoping to spot. A checkout-step test (lots of starters, a few thousand per version) can give you an answer in weeks. A sitewide test on a ~1% conversion rate needs tens of thousands of visitors per version and may never give a clear answer on a smaller store. So check you've got the traffic *before* you promise a test will tell you anything.

## Failure Modes (the ways this goes wrong)

- **Peeking and stopping early** while the result is still bouncing around → you crown winners that aren't real. Fix: set your sample size and end date up front, and don't look early.
- **Conversion lift that's really just a discount** → conversion up, profit per visit down. Fix: judge every test on revenue per visitor and profit, never raw conversion.
- **Testing during a sale or seasonal spike** → the result is contaminated. Fix: don't start tests across Black Friday, big launches, or sales.
- **Ignoring returns** → an apparel "win" that quietly raises returns destroys profit. Fix: count conversion *after returns come out*.
- **Tweaking the homepage or product page** when the real leak is shipping cost or out-of-stock items. Fix: diagnose the whole journey first.
- **Treating blended conversion or total revenue as the scoreboard** → flat sales on *rising* traffic actually means each visitor is worth less. Fix: track revenue per visitor, split by group.

## Edge Cases (when the usual play doesn't fit)

- **Big-ticket, considered buys** (furniture, jewellery): a low conversion rate is *normal* — people take their time across several visits and devices. Don't optimise for buying in one sitting; optimise the nudges that bring them back and help them decide.
- **Subscription / refill businesses:** the first-order conversion rate matters less than **how many people start a subscription and how many stick.** Optimise those.
- **Low-traffic stores (low tens of thousands of visits a month or fewer):** A/B testing just won't give you a trustworthy answer. Use before-and-after comparisons, or just ship a solid best-practice checkout and move on.
- **B2B / wholesale:** "checkout" is really a quote request — optimise the quote-to-close journey, not the cart.

## Worked Example

> **Brief (made up):** Shopify skincare store. 60k visits/mo, blended conversion 1.1%, average order $42, gross margin ~70%, ~70% of traffic on phones. "Sales are flat."
>
> **First move — split it, don't trust the blend.** Pull conversion by device and by new vs. returning. Say desktop checkout→purchase is 55% but **phone is 38%**, and phones are 70% of traffic. The "flat sales" is a phone checkout leak, not a traffic problem — and it was completely hidden inside that blended 1.1%.
>
> **Diagnosis:** Phone checkout→purchase of 38% sits well below this store's own 55% on desktop. On phones the usual culprits are sticker shock at the final step plus a long form. Closing half that gap (38%→~46%) on the 70% phone share is worth roughly +0.2 points of blended conversion ≈ ~120 extra orders/mo ≈ ~$5k revenue ≈ **~$3.5k profit a month** at 70% margin.
>
> **Fixes (ranked by money):** (1) Put express/wallet pay (Shop Pay, Apple Pay) above the fold on phones — kills the long-form problem; watch phone checkout→purchase. (2) Show the shipping cost on the cart page, not at the final step — attacks the #1 reason people bail (surprise costs). (3) Add a $50 free-shipping threshold — *but check the math first*: at a $42 average order and 70% margin, the threshold nudges orders toward $50, but you're eating shipping on the orders that clear it; only roll it out if the profit per order, after the shipping you cover, still comes out positive. The threshold sits ~15–20% above the current average order, which is a sound instinct.
>
> **A/B test:** Express-pay-first vs. current, **on phone checkout-starters only** (the group with enough volume). Main metric: phone checkout→purchase, sanity-checked against revenue per visitor after returns. With ~60k visits/mo, phone checkout-starters number a few thousand a month — at a 38% baseline this gives you an answer in ~2–3 weeks. (A sitewide test at 1.1% would *not* give you a clear read here.) Don't start it during a promo.

The diagnosis splits the numbers before sizing anything, sizes the prize in profit rather than flashy revenue, and runs the test on the only group big enough to prove it.
