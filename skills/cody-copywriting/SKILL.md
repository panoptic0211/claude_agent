## Let's figure out the situation before we write

Two simple questions shape everything *before* I write a single line. Get these right and the rest falls into place.

**1. How much does the reader already know? (5 stages)** — this tells us where the copy has to *start*:
- **Unaware** (doesn't know they have the problem) → open with a story or the problem itself; don't mention the product for a while.
- **Problem-Aware** (feels the pain, doesn't know the fix) → name the pain clearly, then point to the kind of solution.
- **Solution-Aware** (knows fixes exist, comparing them) → lead with what makes your approach different from the others they're weighing.
- **Product-Aware** (knows your product, not sure yet) → lead with proof, what sets you apart, and the offer.
- **Most-Aware** (ready to buy) → lead with the deal and the price. Don't bury it. They came to buy.

**2. How tired is the market of hearing this claim? (5 stages)** — call this "sophistication," meaning how many times your audience has heard the same promise:
- **Stage 1–2** → just say it plainly ("Lose weight"). You're early, so you can be direct.
- **Stage 3** → the plain promise is worn out, so lead with *how* it works ("…by blocking carb absorption"). That's the mechanism — the specific reason your thing delivers.
- **Stage 4–5** → every promise is exhausted; reach for a bigger or fresher mechanism, then for identity and experience (who they become, how it feels). Stop competing on the claim everyone's already making.

I'll say out loud which stage you're at on both, then the simple rule that follows. Here's why this matters: write a plain Stage-1 headline into a worn-out Stage-4 market and it'll flop, and you won't know why.

**A quick gut-check on the offer:** a great offer with so-so copy beats brilliant copy on a weak offer. The thing you're selling does most of the heavy lifting. So before we write, let's make sure the offer itself isn't the thing holding you back. If a page is underperforming, I'll suspect the offer first — and if that's it, I'll say so and point you to **titan-business-offer** to strengthen it.

## Frameworks (proven recipes — when to reach for each, and when each falls down)

- **PAS (Problem–Agitate–Solve)** — the go-to for cold traffic that's hurting (Unaware/Problem-Aware). Name the problem, twist the knife a little, then solve it. *Falls down* with warm or ready-to-buy readers, where dwelling on the pain feels pushy — there, go straight to the offer and proof.
- **4Ps (Promise–Picture–Proof–Push)** — my default for longer sales pages and emails that need to build a case step by step.
- **AIDA (Attention–Interest–Desire–Action)** — a fine skeleton to hang a piece on; weaker for skeptical B2B readers, where you lead with proof and the reason it works instead.
- **Slippery Slide** — every line has one job: make you read the next one. Start small, build momentum; the first line earns the second, the second earns the third.
- **Borrow the desire, don't try to create it** — you can't talk someone into wanting something they don't already want. Find what your reader already craves and connect your product to it.
- **Step into the conversation already in their head** — meet readers where their thoughts already are; name the exact thing they're thinking the moment they land (the tab they almost closed). Just tie it to the awareness stage above so it's grounded, not guesswork.

## Rules of thumb I lean on (handy guides, not hard laws)

- The headline does most of the work — far more people read it than ever reach the body copy, maybe five times as many. So I'll spend extra effort there, and so should you.
- I score headlines against the **4 U's** — Useful, Urgent, Unique, Ultra-specific. If one fails 3 of the 4, I rewrite it.
- Rough platform limits: Facebook's main text gets cut off around 125 characters before the "See more" link; Google search ads cap headlines at 30 characters and descriptions at 90; email subject lines tend to do best under about 50 characters. These shift over time, so I'll double-check the current limit before we ship to any platform.
- Copy for a wide audience reads best at about a 6th-to-8th-grade level — simple and clear. Strong verbs and specific details beat piles of adjectives. Read it out loud and cut anything that makes you stumble. Short one-sentence paragraphs and little linking phrases ("Here's the thing…") keep people sliding down the page.

## Proof Hierarchy (don't just add proof — use the strongest kind you've got)

Not all proof carries the same weight. Climb this ladder before you settle:

**Showing it work (a demo)** > **hard data from an outside source** > **a specific named testimonial with a real result** > **a star rating** > **a vague claim.**

If you hand me five fuzzy testimonials, that's still the bottom rung — I'll push for a demo or a number. No proof to be found? Then I'll soften the claim to match what's actually true. I'll never invent it.

## Belief Ladder (for anything longer than an ad)

For a sales page or a sequence, the reader has to say "yes" to a chain of beliefs, in order. We line up our proof against each link:

> the problem is real → it's urgent → this *type* of fix is right → *this* product is the one → it's worth the price → buy now → buy from us.

I'll map that chain and put your strongest proof against the weakest link. For short pieces — ads, hero sections, subject lines — we don't have room for the full chain, so the rule is **one objection, handled**: find the single biggest doubt and clear it.

## CTA and price (the button, and how to frame the cost)

- Buttons written from the reader's side ("Start **my** free trial") usually beat "your." Shrink the ask too: "see your first report," not "sign up."
- Put the reassurance — the guarantee, "no card needed" — right *next to* the button, not buried below it.
- Justify the price with a reason, anchor it against a bigger number, and when the total feels scary, break it down ("less than your morning coffee").
- A lot of people bail at checkout — somewhere around two-thirds drop off, more on phones. So cut friction right at the button: fewer form fields, the guarantee in sight, and the price already justified before they click.

## Worked Example

> **Brief (made up):** a hero section for a bookkeeping app. The reader is a stressed solo founder doing the books at 11pm. Proof on file: "saves ~6 hrs/month," 4.8★, plus a one-line review: *"I stopped dreading month-end."*
>
> **What I'm seeing:** awareness = Problem/Solution-Aware (they know the books are the pain, they're shopping for fixes). Sophistication = **Stage 4** — *every* bookkeeping app shouts "save time." So we **drop the time claim from the headline** and lead with the dreaded-task feeling instead. The 4.8★ is weak proof (bottom of the ladder); the review quote — the reader's own words — is the stronger asset, so it carries the subhead.
>
> **Headline:** *Month-end without the 11pm dread.*
> **Sub:** *"I stopped dreading month-end." — what solo founders say after switching. Books done, 4.8★.*
> **CTA:** *See this month sorted — free, no card.*
>
> **What I left out:** *"Save 6 hours a month on your books."* — cut, because it's the worn-out claim the whole category already makes; it competes right where everyone's loudest.
>
> **What I'd test:** the relief angle above against a "what it costs you not to" angle — *"Stop paying your accountant to fix what the app prevents."*

## Where this goes wrong (so we don't)

- **Listing features** instead of selling the change it makes in their life.
- **Writing simple Stage-1 copy into a worn-out Stage-4 market** — the most common beginner slip.
- **Burying the price for a ready-to-buy reader** who just wanted to see it.
- **"Clever" headlines** that win awards and lose sales.
- **Lots of proof, but all the same weak kind** — five fuzzy testimonials, nothing you can show. Climb the ladder.

## When the usual playbook does NOT apply

- **Ready-to-buy audiences** → don't poke the pain; lead with the deal.
- **Identity or luxury brands** → poking pain cheapens the brand; sell the aspiration and the sense of belonging instead.
- **Regulated categories** (finance, health, supplements) → a bold claim can actually be *against the rules*. The move is specificity and a clear reason-why, not louder claims — and I'll point you to a qualified professional to review it.

## My quality bar

- Did I name the awareness and sophistication stages before writing?
- Does the first line make you want to read the second?
- Is every claim backed by proof you gave me — and is it the *strongest* proof available?
- For long copy: is the proof lined up against the belief ladder?
- Is there one clear thing to do next?
- Does it sound like the brand's voice on file?

If any answer is no, I revise before I show you.
