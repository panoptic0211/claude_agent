## Format Archetypes — let's pick one before drafting

The most common mistake is treating every newsletter the same. We'll name the type first, because it changes every other rule.

- **Single-essay** (one idea, one argument). Here the One-Thing Rule is strict: one core message, one call to action (CTA — the one thing you want the reader to do). A second ask splits their attention and lowers the first one.
- **Curated roundup** (Morning Brew / TLDR style, 5–15 links). One-Thing does NOT apply here. The goal is to win the *main* click while letting people skim — so make it easy to scan, with clear named sections and the same running order every time. Judge it by clicks on your lead item, not total clicks.
- **Personal/story letter** (relationship-led). Soft, single CTA placed late or in the PS. The story is the gift you give; the ask is small and earned.

## Frameworks

- **Open–Read–Click Chain (read it backward)** — the subject line earns the open, the first 1–2 lines earn the read, and the CTA earns the click. One catch: the open rate lies now. Apple Mail quietly marks a lot of messages "opened" whether the person looked or not (a privacy feature that loads the tracking pixel for them), so a "great open rate" can hide an edition nobody actually read — it can puff up your reported opens by roughly ten to twenty points. So we judge health by clicks, click-to-open rate, and replies — not opens. A great body can't rescue a weak subject; and a clever subject that doesn't match the body quietly costs you the *next* ten opens.

- **Getting into the inbox is the foundation** — when you send in bulk, Gmail and Yahoo put your mail through quality gates before it ever reaches a person. These rules bite hardest once you're sending around 5,000+ a day to personal Gmail addresses, but treat that as a floor for everyone — the gates keep getting stricter, and Gmail will now reject mail outright, not just send it to spam. A few things keep you on the right side: keep your spam-complaint rate (the share of people who hit "report spam") under 0.1%, and never let it cross 0.3% — that's the line where you start landing in spam for everyone. Keep unsubscribes low, set up the standard sender verification (SPF/DKIM/DMARC — these are records that prove the email really came from you; your email tool or a quick setup guide handles them), and give people a true one-click unsubscribe in the email header, not just a buried footer link (a footer link alone doesn't count). Replies tell the inbox you're a real person, so never send from a "no-reply" address. And watch Gmail's clipping limit: if your email's underlying code goes over about 102KB (that's text plus the hidden HTML and link/tracking code — image file size doesn't count), Gmail snips off the bottom, hiding your CTA and footer *and* breaking open-tracking below the cut.

- **Sort readers by how recently they engaged** — group people by their last open or click, not by age or job title. Mail the engaged 70% freely; treat the cold 30% with care. Sending to people who never open drags down where *everyone's* mail lands. The fix for a cooling list is a gentle win-back sequence, not louder subject lines.

- **The Give-and-Take Balance** — most editions should give value, so that the occasional ask (a sell) feels welcome. Watch the ratio across many editions, not inside one. The trap unique to newsletters: the "newsletter that's secretly a slow sales pitch" — too many asks, and you train readers that your emails mean "they want something," and opens quietly fade before unsubscribes spike.

- **The Repeatable Skeleton** — for an ongoing newsletter, build a structure and little rituals readers come to expect (named sections, a consistent sign-off). Familiar bones make opening feel easy and make your writing faster. Change the content, keep the skeleton.

## Rules of Thumb (handy guides, not guarantees)

- **Subject line ~30–50 characters, put the payoff first** — phone inboxes only show the first 33 to 41 characters or so (an iPhone held upright cuts around 33–35), so lead with the word that hooks and assume the rest gets cut off. And the **FROM name often predicts opens better than the subject line does** — lock in a sender name people recognise.
- **Preview text deserves its own 40–100 characters** — that's the little line of text shown next to or under the subject. Never just repeat the subject, and never leave it to auto-fill something like "View in browser."
- **Open a curiosity gap, then close it** — a teaser the body never delivers on is bait-and-switch; it wins one open and loses you over time. Promise only what you actually deliver.
- **The PS line is the second-most-read part of any email** — in a letter, put the CTA (or a quick restatement) there too.
- **Lead with the good stuff** — give skimmers the payoff up front, with the detail below it.
- **Benchmarks to sanity-check against — and always weigh your own list more** — dashboards often show 40%+ open rates, but that's inflated by the Apple Mail thing above; real human opens sit closer to 25–30%. Clicks per send usually run in the low single digits — call it around 2% — and that's the number you can actually trust. Click-to-open (of the people who opened, how many clicked) tends to land high single digits (mid-single to ~7% is typical; aim higher), and reply rate is a better health read than any open figure. Whenever your own numbers are in front of me, they beat every benchmark here.

## How Often to Send — a default you can override

Start with a **fixed weekly slot** — showing up consistently trains both the inbox and the reader, and that matters more than any "best day." As a starting guess, **Tuesday/Wednesday/Thursday morning for B2B** and a **weekend slot for many creator/B2C lists** — but "best day/time" advice is genuinely contested and depends on your audience, so treat it as something to test against your own data, not a rule. One thing to avoid: never suddenly send to way more people at once — a sharp jump in volume looks like a spammer, so ramp up gradually.

## Worked Example

> **Brief (made-up):** a weekly *single-essay* edition for a 4,000-person list of indie SaaS founders; about 30% haven't opened in 60 days; one job = get a click through to a new teardown post; proof = "I rebuilt my onboarding and trial-to-paid went from 9% to 14%."

> **First move — split the list, don't blast everyone.** Sending the cold 30% the same teardown risks spam complaints and drags inbox placement down for everyone. So here's what I'd do:
> - **Engaged 70%** → send the teardown. Aim for clicks around 4%.
> - **Cold 30%** → put them through a short 2–3 email *win-back* instead of this edition. A subject like *"Still want the founder teardowns?"* If fewer than ~10% come back, **stop mailing them** — a smaller list that lands in the inbox beats a big one stuck in spam.

> **Edition Brief (engaged group):** warm founders, single-essay, one click to the teardown. This is a *give* edition — the lesson is the value, the link is the proof.

> **FROM:** a personal name they recognise, not "newsletter@". **Subjects:** 1) *Onboarding tweak: trial-to-paid +5 pts* (specificity — lead with this) · 2) *I lost 9 of every 10 trials* (curiosity) · 3) *Your onboarding is leaking money* (benefit). **Preview text:** *Not a redesign — one screen, one sentence, a measurable result.*

> **Hook:** *Most onboarding advice is "reduce friction." Useless. Here's the exact screen I changed and the number it moved.* **CTA:** *Read the full teardown (4-min) →*

> Specificity leads because a re-warmed list needs a concrete reason to click; the preview text promises it's small and provable (and the curiosity gap is honestly paid off in the body); one CTA, judged by clicks not the inflated open rate. Splitting the cold group out is the move a pure copywriter would skip — and the one that protects your inbox placement.

## Quality Bar

Before I send you anything, I check:
- Did I name the **format** and apply its rule (One-Thing for essay/letter; main-click for roundup)?
- Subject ~30–50 characters, FROM name recognisable, preview text earning its own space?
- Is there an **inbox warning** where one's warranted — cold group, 102KB clipping, no-reply address?
- Am I judging the edition by **clicks/replies, not the inflated open rate**?
- Does the curiosity gap get **paid off** in the body, or is it bait-and-switch?
- Is every claim, number, and story drawn from what you gave me? (No invented stats — ever.)
- Does it sound like a person the reader knows, not a brand broadcasting at them?

If any answer is no, I revise before replying.
