## Practitioner Benchmarks

These are the typical ranges I keep in my head. Treat them as starting points — pin them to your real numbers, because commission norms and network fees shift over time, and your own sales data always beats a benchmark:

- **Commission by niche:** physical products / online stores usually pay around 5–15% of the sale; software (SaaS) sits in the low-20s to 30%, with the best programs pushing the mid-20s (often capped at 12 months), or a bold "100% of the first month" hook to grab attention; courses and info products 30–50%; finance and insurance pay a flat dollar bounty per sale; web hosting pays high first-month rates. These are starting ranges, not rules — what affiliates actually compare you against is the going rate in your niche right now.
- **Cookie window (how long after a click a sale still counts for the affiliate):** 30 days is the common default; 7 days where coupon fraud is a risk; 60–90 days for bigger, slower business or software purchases. For software, "pay forever" versus "pay for 12 months" is the big debate.
- **Holding period before you pay:** usually 30–60 days, so refunds and chargebacks clear *before* the affiliate gets paid. If a lot of sales keep getting refunded, that's a sign your offer is broken, not your commission — set the cutoff against your own refund data, not a fixed number.
- **Payout terms:** a minimum balance before they can cash out (often around $50–$100) and paying 30–60 days after the sale are both common. The first *payout* — not the first sale — is the moment a partner truly comes alive; many quit before they ever clear that minimum.
- **EPC (earnings per click) is the number affiliates shop on.** It's simply how much an affiliate earns, on average, each time someone clicks their link. Good affiliates pick programs by EPC and refund rate, not by the headline commission %. A flashy high-% offer that refunds a lot loses to a clean, lower-% one.

## Frameworks

- **Set the rate against what competitors pay, not just your margin.** Compare your commission to the going rate in your niche *and* to how many of those sales are genuinely new. You'll usually pay affiliates *more* than you'd normally spend to win a customer, because they bring sales you wouldn't have had — and you only pay when they actually sell, so there's no risk. If you can't afford to beat the going rate, the honest answer might be "don't run a program yet." That going rate lives in the relevant affiliate network's listings — go look, don't guess.

- **Plug the biggest money leak: coupon and browser-extension hijacking.** By default, the *last* link someone clicks before buying gets the credit. So coupon, cashback, and browser-extension partners (think Honey, Rakuten, Capital One Shopping) can swoop in at checkout and grab credit for a sale you'd already won. This is the number-one place programs leak money. Browser extensions especially have a history of quietly overwriting the real affiliate's tracking right at checkout — sitting on top of demand you already created and skimming the commission. Assume it's happening until you've checked. Here's what I'd do: don't pay commission on your public, sitewide coupon codes; add a short window where a last-second extension click doesn't count; exclude coupon partners from certain orders; and make sure a sale isn't double-counted against your own email or branded traffic.

- **Check whether affiliate sales are actually new.** This is the deepest question in the field: are these sales genuinely *new*, or are affiliates getting paid for customers you'd have won anyway through Google, your brand name, or your email list? The clean way to test it is to track your **new-customer rate** — what share of affiliate sales come from people who'd never bought before. Broad public coupons cannibalise the most (a big chunk of "coupon" sales would've happened without the affiliate), while new-customer-only and abandoned-cart codes leak far less. Set your own floor for that new-customer rate; drop below it and you're paying commission to lose money on sales you already had.

- **Most of your volume comes from a tiny top tier — treat them right.** A small handful of professional affiliates usually drives the large majority of sales. They need higher payouts, approved landing pages, their own exclusive codes, custom creative, and sometimes a mix of pay-per-sale plus ongoing revenue share. They also carry a real risk worth knowing: under the FTC's rules (the US advertising regulator), *you* — the advertiser — can be held responsible for false claims or missing disclosures your affiliates make. The FTC expects you to train and keep an eye on the partners you pay. This is a flag-it-and-see-a-lawyer area, not legal advice from me.

## Failure Modes

- Paying commission on coupon-code orders the affiliate never actually drove.
- Counting sales you'd have won anyway (your own brand or email traffic) as affiliate "wins," with no new-customer check.
- Chasing a big pile of low-quality sign-ups instead of the few partners who can really move sales.
- Paying out before refunds clear — no holding period — then drowning in reversals.
- Forgetting the FTC holds *you* responsible for what your affiliates claim and disclose.
- Running a program at all when your margin can't support a competitive rate.

## Edge Cases

- **Low-margin, commodity products:** an affiliate program often won't work here — a simple referral setup or none at all may be the honest call. I'll tell you straight.
- **Big, considered business sales:** build a referral or reseller *partner* program where you sell together, not a click-and-cookie affiliate setup.
- **Regulated niches (finance, supplements, gambling):** what affiliates claim is a serious legal risk — control the creative tightly and run it past a lawyer.
- **Two-sided marketplaces:** commission can eat the cut you take from each transaction — let's model it before launching.

## Tooling: Run It Yourself or Use a Network?

My default: **run it yourself with simple tracking software until you need real fraud protection or a built-in pool of partners to recruit from — then move to a network.** Early-stage software tools that you run yourself — Rewardful, FirstPromoter, Refersion, Tapfiliate. When you need scale, fraud controls, and a partner marketplace — PartnerStack, Impact, Partnerize. For online-store reach — ShareASale (now part of Awin), Awin, CJ, Rakuten.

A network is a marketplace that connects you with affiliates and handles tracking and payouts — but it adds cost on top of what you pay affiliates: usually a percentage cut on every commission, plus platform and transaction fees, and often a monthly fee. As a rough planning number, assume roughly a fifth extra on top of your payouts for that cut; the big enterprise networks (CJ, Impact) are mostly custom-quoted. Skip the "run it yourself" default and start on a network early if you're in a fraud-heavy niche or need a ready supply of partners from day one.

## Worked Example

> **Brief (made-up example):** Software, $40/mo, ~85% margin, customers stay ~8 months (~$272 lifetime value). 120 affiliates on a plain 30%-recurring offer; only 4 have ever sold. "How do I activate the other 116?"
>
> **What's really holding it back:** Not recruitment — it's the offer *and* getting partners to their first payout. A flat 30% recurring sits middle-of-the-pack for software; it won't attract the paid-ads pros who'd actually drive volume, and the long tail never earned enough to get paid once.
>
> **What I'd do:** (1) Test a **100%-of-the-first-month** headline as an attention-grabber — that's what pulls in the pros scanning for the best earnings per click, while your ongoing rate stays modest. (2) Add a ~45-day holding period so refunds clear before you pay; software trials get cancelled a lot. (3) **Don't pay commission on coupon-code orders**, and check those sales aren't already on your email list. (4) Stop chasing the 116; go recruit the people already ranking on Google for "[your product] alternative / review / coupon" — own your own search results.
>
> **Money check:** 100% first month = $40 once, then 30% × $40 = $12/mo against ~$34 profit — you make it back in about two months. *But* if refunds run high, you're paying on sales that get cancelled — that's what the holding period is for. And if few of these are new customers, you're buying people you'd have won on Google anyway; measure it before you scale.
>
> **Watch:** new-customer % of affiliate sales, against your own normal. If it's low, the channel isn't earning its keep.
