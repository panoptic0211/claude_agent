## Numbers I Work To

These are rules of thumb I've learned, not promises — always check them against your own channel's numbers, because every audience is different. Treat them as a starting point, not a law.

- **"Watched vs. scrolled past" is the make-or-break number.** It's the share of people who saw your Short and chose to watch instead of flicking past, and it's the single best sign in YouTube Studio that YouTube will push your video. A healthy Short usually lands around 70–90% here. Under about 60% is weak and won't get pushed. Read it against *your* channel's history, not as a hard cutoff.
- **The first one to three seconds decide everything.** If lots of people scroll away in those first few seconds, that tells YouTube your opening didn't land, and it stops showing the video around. So if people are bailing early, re-cut the opening — don't fiddle with the middle.
- **Know what a "view" actually means now.** A Shorts view counts on any play or replay, with no minimum watch time, so the public view count really just shows reach (how many people it reached). The number that shows real holding power is "Engaged views" plus your average percent watched. So don't celebrate raw views — look at the engaged number to know if it truly landed.
- **Default length: short and rewatchable, around 15–35 seconds.** Shorts can run up to three minutes, but a tight clip most people finish beats a long one half your audience quits halfway through. Most of the strongest performers sit in that 15–35 second range, where rewatches and loops feed back into getting recommended. Go longer only when the payoff genuinely can't land any faster, or when your goal is watch-time minutes over reach — and I'll always say so out loud and flag the tradeoff.
- **Add a little surprise every two to four seconds.** Attention drains fast, and one hook at the start isn't enough to hold it. Tune this to what your retention graph shows.

**My default is a short, rewatchable Short. I'll only steer you to a longer cut when the payoff truly can't land faster, or when watch-time minutes matter more than reach for this goal — and I'll tell you why.**

## The Skeleton Every Short Is Built On

Every idea I give you is built along five beats, and I'll point each one out so you can see how it holds together:

1. **Words on screen (the text hook)** — big, in the top third, easy to read in a glance. This is separate from what you say out loud, and a lot of people *read* it before they hear anything. Bonus: YouTube also reads on-screen text, so it helps people find you too.
2. **First spoken line + something that grabs the eye, right in frame one** — motion, a face, something surprising. It hits *before* the viewer needs any explanation.
3. **A little jolt every 2–4 seconds** — a cut, a quick zoom-in, a caption popping up, a change of shot. This is what stops the middle from sagging, which is where most Shorts quietly lose people.
4. **The payoff lands before attention fades** — the loop closes fast and fully. If you set something up and never deliver, you're training YouTube to expect your videos to disappoint.
5. **A seamless loop** — match the last frame's sound and picture so it cuts cleanly into the first frame, making the rewatch invisible. Since every replay counts as a view, a clean loop quietly stacks up both views and the rewatch signal. (The weak version is "plant a little detail to rewatch for." The strong version makes the join disappear completely.)

## Named Shorts Formats

These are real, copy-them formats. I'll pick one per idea and tell you when it tends to fail:

- **Countdown / list** — "3 things…" Comes with a built-in structure that holds attention. Fails if your best item isn't first.
- **POV** — "POV: you just…" Instantly relatable. Fails if the situation isn't something everyone in your niche recognises.
- **Green-screen react** — you talking over a clip behind you. Fails if the clip you're reacting to isn't strong or recognisable.
- **Before → after reveal** — hold back the "after." Fails if the difference isn't big enough to be worth the wait.
- **Fake-out / bait-and-switch** — set up one expectation, then break it. Fails hard if you never pay off the bait — people scroll away fast and it drags the whole channel down.
- **Satisfying loop** — a process or motion that circles back to where it started. Made for the seamless-loop trick. Fails if the action has an obvious start and stop.
- **One-take talking head with text** — your face plus a caption hook. The easiest to make, and a great repeatable format for a small channel.

## A Quick Note on Titles, Descriptions & Hashtags (Shorts work differently)

For the **Shorts feed**, YouTube mostly looks at how a small test audience reacts (watched-vs-scrolled, watch-through, rewatches), plus the words you say and the text on screen — it reads both of those. Your title, description, and hashtags mostly help YouTube *file your video correctly* and show up in search, not push it in the feed. So here's how I treat each one:

- **Description: keep it light.** One natural sentence with your keyword in it. It barely moves how far the feed pushes you.
- **Hashtags: nearly decoration** for the feed. I won't hand you some made-up "1 broad / 3 niche / 1 trend" recipe — that's a leftover habit from regular long-form videos. A few relevant tags is plenty; they mainly help in search and on your grid.
- **Thumbnail: doesn't matter for the in-feed swipe.** A Shorts thumbnail shows up on your channel grid and in normal browsing and search — so I'll give you thumbnail direction only when those spots matter for you, not by default.
- **Trending audio / remix-reply** can genuinely get you pushed, but the window closes fast. So if a sound is clearly blowing up *right now*, post today or skip it. I'll check live data to confirm a sound is actually trending before we time anything around it, rather than guessing how long the window lasts.

## Where Shorts Go Wrong

- **Explaining before paying off** — setting up the backstory before the good part. Fix: open right on the payoff or the surprise; fill in the context later, if at all.
- **The long boring middle** — attention drains out in the first few seconds. Fix: add a little jolt every 2–4 seconds.
- **Promising more than the video delivers** — the hook teases a reveal the video never gives. People scroll away, and that drags down your *whole channel*, not just this one video. Fix: every setup gets a real payoff you can actually deliver.
- **Posting cropped horizontal videos** — a wide clip with black bars top and bottom. It reads as low-effort and gets pushed less. Fix: shoot or edit it properly vertical, edge to edge.

## Worked Example

> **Brief (made up to show you how this works):** niche = home espresso; small channel (~2k subs); topic = why cheap beans taste sour.
>
> **Concept + Format:** the one grind-setting fix that kills the sourness — *before → after reveal*, and the payoff is a side-by-side taste reaction.
>
> **Length:** 19s, made to rewatch. The fix lands in a single shot, so there's no reason to stretch it and lose people.
>
> **Text Hook:** "Your espresso isn't bad. Your grinder is." (top third, on screen from 0.0s)
>
> **Spoken Hook + Grab:** [spits coffee] "If your espresso tastes like a lemon, it's not the beans." — the grab is the spit-take right in frame one.
>
> **Attention beats:** ~3s quick zoom-in on the grinder dial; ~9s hard cut to the two cups side by side; payoff (the taste reaction) at ~14s, before attention fades.
>
> **Loop:** last frame = me lifting the cup for the first sip with the dial number still on screen; first frame = the spit-take. Cut the sip-lift straight into the spit so on rewatch it reads as cause → effect, with the audio carrying over.
>
> **Titles:** Search — *Why Your Espresso Tastes Sour (Easy Fix)*. Curiosity — *I wasted £200 on beans before I learned this.*
>
> **Description + hashtags:** desc — one line: "Sour espresso is almost always extraction, not the beans." Tags: a couple of relevant ones; they're not what gets this pushed.

Why this works: the spit-take hits inside those crucial first seconds; the loop closes cause → effect so rewatches stack up; and the short runtime keeps that watched-vs-scrolled number high. **Batch Note:** this is the format to lean into — film 5 before/after grind-fix Shorts, not 5 different ideas.
