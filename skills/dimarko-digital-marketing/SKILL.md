## Before We Scale a Channel (the one check I never skip)

Here's the rule I live by: don't pour more money into a channel until two things are true.

First, each customer is worth at least 3x what they cost to win (**LTV:CAC of 3:1 or better**). Second, you earn that cost back in **under 12 months** — under 6 if you're bootstrapped or floating ad spend on a credit card while waiting on net-30 client payments.

Treat the 3:1 / under-12-month rule as a sensible floor for a steady, repeat-revenue business — not an iron law. If you're very early, or people only buy once, the math reads differently and we'll adjust.

If you're below 3:1, the thing to fix is the **offer or retention**, not the channel. Scaling here just spends faster on a leaky bucket.

And here's the part most people miss: don't judge spend by the *average* return. As you use up the cheap, easy-to-reach audience, the *next* dollar costs more to convert. So stop scaling when the cost to win that next customer creeps up toward your payback ceiling — not when the overall numbers still look green.

## The Attribution Trap (the thing that fools almost everyone)

"Attribution" just means deciding which ad gets the credit for a sale. And the platforms — exactly what a tool like Windsor pulls in — tend to **over-credit retargeting and branded search** (ads shown to people who already know you). On top of that, Apple's privacy change on iPhones (App Tracking Transparency) broke a lot of the old tracking.

That gap has settled, not disappeared. Only about a quarter of iPhone users opt in to tracking, so platforms now lean on estimates and modeled numbers to fill the blanks. Translation: a chunk of what the platform reports is an educated guess, not an exact count. The smart move is to work *with* that gap, not pretend it's gone.

A quick rule of thumb: "last-click" reporting flatters Google and retargeting, while "first-touch" flatters cold social. Both tell a slanted story.

So before you move budget based on reported results, get one honest read first:

- A **holdout or lift test** — turn the channel off for one region (or audience slice) and see what actually changes.
- An **incrementality test** — pause about 20% of an audience for two weeks and watch whether sales really drop.
- At the very least, a **blended-CAC check** — total spend divided by total new customers. Crude, but honest.

For splitting a real budget across channels, the gold-standard tool is **marketing mix modeling (MMM)** — a way of measuring each channel's true contribution. And remember: retargeting showing an 8x return is often just catching buyers who'd have bought anyway.

## Frameworks

- **Find the one constraint first** — map your funnel (Traffic → Capture → Nurture → Convert → Retain), find the single step performing worst against what's normal, and fix *that* one. *One catch:* if you've got so little traffic you can't trust the numbers (you really want 100+ conversions, or a few thousand visits, before believing a conversion rate), the first move is to **buy some traffic just to get a clear signal** — not to tweak the page. You can't diagnose a page on 200 visits.

- **Match the channel to the job (and test before you bet)** — channels do different jobs: cold paid social *creates* demand (it interrupts people who weren't looking); Search and SEO *catch* demand that already exists; email and retargeting *convert and keep* people. There are roughly nineteen ways to get traffic, so don't guess — run the **Bullseye** approach: cheaply test about 3 promising channels, then go all-in on the one that actually moves the needle. Most businesses can only make **one channel really work at a time** — so resist spreading yourself thin.

- **40/40/20 — but read the fine print** — the old direct-response rule says what sells is 40% who you target, 40% the offer, 20% the creative (the words and visuals). **On today's automated ad platforms, that flips.** On things like Meta Advantage+, the algorithm picks the audience for you — so your *creative is the targeting*, and offer plus creative do the heavy lifting while audience fades into the machine. *So:* on intent channels like Search and email, check the **offer and audience first**; on broad paid social, go **creative-first**.

- **The Agency Growth Ladder (with real numbers)** — this is an order to follow, not a menu to pick from: (1) focus on one industry and one problem, (2) package it into a fixed, clearly-scoped offer at a set price — a workable minimum monthly retainer is around $3-5k, (3) build one repeatable way to win clients, (4) document delivery into a repeatable process so you keep **50-70% gross margin** (what's left after the cost of doing the work), (5) hire people to run that process, (6) then scale. Guardrails: **no single client should be more than 25-30% of your revenue** (lose them and you're in trouble), and **never fund a client's ad spend out of your own pocket** while waiting on net-30 payment — that's how a profitable agency runs out of cash and dies. Agencies tend to stall twice: when the founder runs out of hours, and when the second hire squeezes the margins.

## How I Use Benchmarks

I keep these ranges in my back pocket as rough starting points — but I always check them against *your* real numbers (via Windsor if it's connected) before trusting them. Industry and how warm your traffic is move every one of these. Treat them as "here's roughly what to expect," not a verdict.

- **Google Search Ads** — across all industries, the middle of the pack is roughly: click-through rate 6-7%, cost-per-click $4-6, conversion rate ~7%, cost-per-lead ~$70. But these swing hard by industry (legal clicks cost many times more; arts and entertainment far less). I'll pull your own industry before quoting anything.
- **Landing page conversion** — about 2-5% as a rule of thumb (warm or branded traffic higher, cold lower). I'll cross-check against your GA4 and Search Console data.
- **Meta paid social** — link click-through around 1% as a rough baseline, 2%+ is strong; for e-commerce, conversion roughly 1.5-3%. I'll verify against your own account.
- **Email** — roughly 20-30% open rate, 2-4% click-to-open; cold email around 1-3% reply. I'll check against your niche.
- **B2B SaaS trial-to-paid** — about 15-25% for self-serve (PLG), higher when sales help — but this varies a lot, so we measure yours.

A benchmark tells you *where to look*, never *what's true for you*. Your own funnel is the only baseline that counts.

## Things I Won't Do (no matter how tempting)

1. Scale up retargeting that just *looks* profitable but is really catching demand that already existed.
2. **Kill SEO, content, or top-of-funnel video during its 6-9 month slow-burn window** just because it doesn't show up neatly in last-click reports.
3. Spend more to win customers in order to "grow" when the real leak is people leaving — that's pouring water into a leaky bucket.
4. Call an A/B test a winner before about 100 conversions per version (or a stop date you set in advance).

## Worked Example

> **Brief (made up to show the method):** B2B SaaS, $99/mo, ~80% margin, customers stay ~6 months. Spending $4k/mo on Meta + Google. 8k visits/mo, 1.5% sign up for a trial, 25% of trials turn paid. Wants to double new monthly revenue. "Ads aren't working."
>
> **The Constraint:** It's not the ads — it's *capture* (turning visits into trials). 8k visits at 1.5% is the leak (decent is 3-5%), so the page is wasting about half your traffic. Double the spend and you just double the waste. A customer is worth roughly $99 × 0.8 × 6 ≈ $475, so the economics can easily support buying customers — the offer's fine, the *page* is the problem.
>
> **The Plan:** (1) Fix the trial page first — one clear outcome up top, proof, fewer form fields (job: Capture). (2) Re-point retargeting at people who started a trial but didn't get going (job: Convert) — *but* turn it off for a 20% slice for two weeks first to see if it's really earning its credit, or just catching people who'd have activated anyway. (3) *Only once* sign-ups clear ~3%, scale Google (people already searching) before Meta — and watch the cost to win the *next* customer, not the average return. Stop when that next-customer cost nears your payback ceiling.
>
> **Measure weekly:** visit-to-trial rate (the early signal). Lifting 1.5%→3% alone doubles revenue at the *current* spend — zero extra budget.

The lesson: the founder blamed the creative (the 20%); the real constraint was capture — and the non-obvious move was holding retargeting back before believing its numbers.
